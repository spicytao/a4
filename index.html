Interactive Visualization and Society · Assignment 4

Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

This report uses the same Guttmacher Institute state-level abortion dataset to build two opposed visual arguments. The point is not to discover a single neutral truth, but to show how visual rhetoric emerges through metric choice, field construction, filtering, annotation, emphasis, and narrative framing.

Shengtao Shen sstheo@mit.edu A4 Guttmacher Institute Abortion Data by State

Proposition

Proposition

Abortion bans and clinic scarcity do not eliminate abortion need; they mainly shift where abortion care is obtained and therefore where it is recorded.

Visualization 1 — For the Proposition

My first chart is designed to make one pattern feel unavoidable: low abortion rates recorded inside a state do not necessarily mean low abortion demand among that state’s residents. To support the proposition, I foreground the discrepancy between abortions that occurred in a state and abortions obtained by that state’s residents.

Design rationale

How this visualization was made persuasive

This figure persuades by redefining what should count as the relevant measure. A viewer looking only at abortions performed within a state might conclude that abortion restrictions successfully reduced abortion itself. By placing that measure beside the abortion rate among the same state’s residents, I redirect attention from where care was recorded to whether residents still sought care at all.

The chart is persuasive partly because it contains a real analytical insight and partly because it selectively amplifies that insight. The earnest part is the comparison itself: occurrence rate and resident rate are genuinely different measures, and the gap between them matters. The rhetorical part is that I only foreground the states where that difference is dramatic, rank them by gap size, and visually privilege the endpoint I want the viewer to trust most. I am not fabricating a pattern, but I am staging one interpretation so it appears especially coherent.

  • Score: +1.5 Comparing resident abortion rate with in-state abortion rate. This is the central analytical move of the project, and I regard it as mostly earnest. The two measures answer different questions, and putting them in the same frame helps reveal that “where abortions happened” is not the same as “whether residents sought abortions.” The comparison exposes a meaningful policy distinction rather than distorting the data.
  • Score: -1.5 Showing the states with the largest mismatch. I did not show all states with equal weight. I selected the cases where the resident-versus-occurrence gap is strongest, because those states make the proposition most persuasive. This is effective because it concentrates attention on the clearest evidence, but it is also selective because smaller or less supportive cases disappear from view.
  • Score: -1 Sorting by gap size rather than by alphabet or geography. Ordering the states from the largest mismatch downward transforms a set of examples into what feels like a systematic ranked pattern. The move looks innocuous, but it strongly shapes interpretation: the viewer encounters the most dramatic cases first and then reads the rest as confirmation.
  • Score: -0.5 Using a span-based encoding. I chose a slope/dumbbell-like span instead of detached bars or isolated points because it makes the difference between the two rates immediately legible. That is analytically helpful. At the same time, a long horizontal gap also feels dramatic, so the encoding intensifies the emotional force of the discrepancy. I see this as a mixed choice: informative in structure, but rhetorically strong in effect.
  • Score: -1.5 Using color hierarchy and a strongly interpretive title. I made the resident-rate endpoint darker so the viewer’s attention lands first on the measure I want to count as social reality. The title goes further by implying that abortion bans “relocate” care rather than reduce it. That reading is plausible, but it is still an interpretive claim, not a neutral description of the marks alone.

Visualization 2 — Against the Proposition

My second chart argues against the proposition by changing the question. Instead of asking whether abortion care was displaced across state lines, it asks whether residents in high-barrier states still have lower abortion rates overall. To make that argument work, I construct a single calculated field—the Access Burden Score—that compresses several dimensions of restriction into one axis.

Design rationale

How this visualization was made persuasive

This chart persuades by replacing the comparison that powered Figure 1 with a new explanatory structure. In Figure 1, the key story was the mismatch between two abortion measures. In Figure 2, that mismatch disappears. I instead define a burden index and ask the viewer to interpret resident abortion rate as the primary outcome. That shift makes the chart feel cleaner, more causal, and more statistically coherent—even though it depends on my own choices about what should count as “burden.”

Calculated field used in Figure 2 Access Burden Score = 0.5 × (% of women ages 15–44 living in a county without a clinic) + 0.3 × (% of counties without a known clinic) + 0.2 × (% of residents obtaining abortions who traveled out of state)

I weighted the first component most heavily because it reflects how much of the reproductive-age population lacks local clinic access. I weighted county-level clinic absence second because it captures geographic scarcity, but it is less sensitive to where people actually live. I weighted out-of-state travel third because it is partly an outcome of burden rather than a pure precondition, but it still helps intensify the sense that access barriers are materially consequential. This field is therefore both analytical and rhetorical: it summarizes multiple relevant indicators, but it also encodes my own judgment about what forms of restriction should count the most.

  • Score: +0.5 Constructing a weighted Access Burden Score from multiple variables. Rather than relying on one raw indicator, I built a calculated field from three measures: population exposure to clinic absence, county-level clinic scarcity, and out-of-state travel. This is analytically meaningful because abortion access is multidimensional, and a composite index makes that complexity more legible. It is also rhetorical because the weighting scheme is my own design decision rather than something naturally given by the data.
  • Score: -1 Making population exposure the dominant component. By assigning the largest weight to the share of women ages 15–44 living in counties without a clinic, I made the score feel population-centered and therefore more substantively grounded. That choice is defensible, but it also helps the variable appear more authoritative and more persuasive than a purely geographic measure might.
  • Score: -1.5 Using resident abortion rate as the only outcome variable. This is crucial because it removes the resident-versus-occurrence comparison that made relocation visible in Figure 1. Once that frame disappears, the viewer is invited to judge a different relationship: whether residents in high-burden states still show lower abortion rates overall.
  • Score: -1 Adding a regression line. The regression line turns a scattered cloud into an interpretable directional story. It does not falsify the observations, but it stabilizes them into a readable pattern and encourages the viewer to see trend before noise.
  • Score: -1.5 Using quadrant framing and darkening the lower-right region. I intentionally emphasized the lower-right quadrant because it contains the cases that best support the anti-claim: higher burden, lower resident abortion rate. The values are unchanged, but visual hierarchy makes that corner feel like the chart’s conclusion. This is one of the clearest examples of persuasion through emphasis rather than through falsification.

Final Reflection

What changed across these two visualizations was not the dataset itself, but the definition of what counted as evidence. In Figure 1, I treated the gap between resident and in-state abortion rates as the key signal, which made displacement visible and made low in-state counts look incomplete. In Figure 2, I treated resident abortion rate as the main outcome and built a synthetic measure of restriction to explain it. The two arguments therefore diverge not because one is invented and the other is true, but because each chart selectively elevates a different structure in the same data.

The calculated field in Figure 2 made this especially clear to me. It felt sincere because no single variable fully captures abortion access, and combining several indicators into one score can genuinely improve legibility. But it was also unmistakably authored. I had to decide which variables belonged inside the score, which ones did not, and what relative importance each deserved. The field therefore did not merely measure burden; it also expressed my judgment about what burden should mean.

This assignment made me less confident that there is a simple formal line between “persuasive” and “deceptive” visualization. Some of my choices were obviously strategic: filtering cases, ordering them by the strongest pattern, using darker color to direct attention, and writing titles that interpret before the viewer fully inspects the evidence. But other choices felt genuinely informative, such as comparing two different abortion measures or constructing a composite field to summarize multiple dimensions of access. What unsettled me is that the same technique can appear ethical or manipulative depending on how transparently it is explained and how much room it leaves for competing interpretations.

I now think the more useful ethical question is not whether a chart contains rhetoric, because every chart does. The better question is how visible that rhetoric is and how aggressively it forecloses alternative readings. A visualization becomes more troubling when its framing is hidden so well that interpretation begins to feel inevitable. In that sense, ethical visualization may not mean eliminating persuasion. It may mean acknowledging that persuasion is always present and taking responsibility for how selectively, how strongly, and how invisibly it is used.

The following images helped me articulate that idea more clearly. They suggest that visualization is never only a neutral transfer of information. A chart can invite observation, call for action, provoke reaction, organize discussion, or express the social position of its maker. Once I began thinking this way, it became easier to see that my own charts were not passive containers of data. Each one imagined a viewer, structured their attention, and hoped to leave them with a particular conclusion.

My main takeaway from this assignment is that ethical visualization is not the absence of persuasion, but the disciplined use of it. Every act of selection, transformation, and emphasis introduces perspective. The harder and more important task is deciding whether that perspective still leaves enough of the data visible for other interpretations to remain imaginable.

That is why several of my design decisions above are scored as mixed rather than purely deceptive. A calculated field, a composite index, or a comparison between two measures can be both analytically sincere and strategically useful at the same time. The ethical risk emerges when a chart hides the contingency of those choices so effectively that its rhetoric starts to masquerade as inevitability.
