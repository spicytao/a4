Guttmacher Institute Abortion Data by State

Proposition

Proposition

Abortion bans and clinic scarcity do not reduce abortion need; they primarily relocate where abortion care is recorded.

Visualization 1 — For the Proposition

How this chart argues for the proposition: This visualization supports the proposition by framing low in-state abortion rates as incomplete evidence. Instead of asking only how many abortions occur within a state, it compares that number to the abortion rate among people who live in that state. Where the resident rate remains much higher than the in-state rate, the chart suggests that abortion need has not disappeared, but that abortion care has simply become less visible locally and more likely to occur elsewhere.

The figure becomes persuasive because it is not organized neutrally. First, it focuses on a subset of states where the mismatch is especially large, so the reader is immediately confronted with the strongest examples rather than the most representative national average. Second, the chart is ordered so that the most dramatic cases appear first, encouraging the reader to interpret the remaining examples through that same frame. Once Missouri appears as the opening case, the rest of the graphic reads as further confirmation rather than as mixed evidence.

The title also performs substantial rhetorical work. Rather than simply describing a comparison between two rates, it interprets the gap as proof that abortion bans “relocate care.” That is a stronger claim than the marks alone can fully establish, but it gives the viewer a causal explanation before they begin reading the data closely. In that sense, the title turns an observed mismatch into a political conclusion.

Color is another major part of the persuasion. I intentionally made the values I most wanted the viewer to notice darker and more visually dominant. The resident-related endpoint is encoded with the darkest and most saturated color, while the in-state endpoint is visually weaker. This means the eye is naturally pulled toward the resident value first, making it feel like the truer or more important number. That choice strengthens the chart’s argument because it quietly teaches the viewer which metric should count as reality.

The same is true of emphasis more broadly: the most supportive cases are not only sorted first, but also visually intensified. The strongest spans are longer, darker, and more heavily annotated. These choices do not fabricate data, but they do shape what the reader sees first, remembers most, and interprets as central.

This is the most consequential persuasive move because it concentrates the chart around the strongest supporting evidence and suppresses weaker counterexamples.

Visualization 2 — Against the Proposition

How this chart argues against the proposition: This visualization takes the opposite position by changing the question. Instead of asking where abortions are performed, it asks whether states with greater barriers to access still show lower abortion rates among residents. That shift is crucial. Once the in-state versus resident comparison disappears, the displacement argument loses visibility and the overall relationship between barriers and rates becomes the dominant reading.

The x-axis uses a composite Access Burden Score, which combines several restrictions into a single variable. This makes the chart look more comprehensive and more analytically authoritative than a single clinic-access measure would. It also creates a stronger sense that the x-axis represents a real and total burden, which helps the anti-position appear more systematic and credible.

The trend line and the quadrant structure do most of the rhetorical work. The regression line organizes a noisy cloud of points into a clean downward story, while the horizontal and vertical average lines divide the chart into simple interpretive zones. This makes the lower-right quadrant feel like a meaningful conclusion: high burden, low resident rate. Once the chart is divided in this way, the reader is naturally guided toward that region as the place where the anti-argument “lives.”

Color and background emphasis intensify that effect. I intentionally wanted viewers to pay special attention to the lower-right quadrant, so I darkened both the points in that region and the background of that quadrant itself. This makes the points there feel more visually important than equally valid points elsewhere in the chart. The viewer’s attention is not spread evenly across the scatterplot; it is pulled toward the part of the chart that most strongly supports the anti-position.

The use of red also matters. Red carries emotional and perceptual weight: it feels urgent, consequential, and heightened. By using a red-based palette and deepening the tones in the lower-right region, I make that area feel more significant and more decisive than the rest of the graph. In other words, color here is not merely descriptive; it is affective. It helps the chart produce a feeling that “something important is happening here,” which strengthens the argument beyond the trend line alone.

Selective labeling extends the same logic. Only a small set of states are named, and they are chosen because they reinforce the structure of the argument: low-burden, high-rate states in the upper-left and high-burden, low-rate states in the lower-right. These labels create narrative anchors that make the chart feel easier to interpret and more conclusive than it actually is.

  • Score: -2 Using only resident abortion rate as the outcome. By removing the in-state versus resident comparison that dominated Figure 1, I suppress the displacement story and redefine what the reader should treat as the meaningful measure of abortion incidence.
  • Score: -1 Constructing a composite Access Burden Score. Combining multiple barriers into a single variable makes the chart feel more comprehensive and more analytically authoritative, even though the score itself is a constructed choice.
  • Score: -1 Adding a regression line. The trend line transforms a noisy cloud into a coherent negative relationship. It gives the reader a ready-made interpretation and reduces the sense of ambiguity in the scatterplot.
  • Score: -1 Using four-quadrant reference lines. The average lines divide the plot into a simple structure and make the lower-right region feel like a natural conclusion zone. This is subtle, but it strongly stabilizes the anti-reading.
  • Score: -1.5 Darkening the lower-right quadrant and its points. I specifically wanted viewers to focus on the high-burden, low-rate region, so I deepened the background tone there and made those points visually stronger. This is a persuasive design move because it channels attention toward the examples that best support the anti-position.
  • Score: -1 Using red as an affective cue. The red palette is not neutral. It creates urgency, intensity, and a stronger emotional impression. That makes the emphasized lower-right points feel more consequential and therefore more convincing.
  • Score: -1 Selective labeling of key states. Only a handful of states are named, and they are chosen because they make the chart’s directional story easier to read. The labels help guide the viewer toward the desired interpretation.
  • Score: -1.5 Title framing the relationship as meaningful persistence. The title pushes the reader to interpret the negative relationship as evidence that barriers “still” matter, even after the possibility of relocation is acknowledged. This is stronger than a neutral variable description.

Final Reflection

What changed between these two visualizations was not the dataset, but the operational definition of what counted as evidence. In the first chart, I treated the mismatch between resident and in-state abortion rates as the central signal, which made displacement visible and made low in-state rates seem incomplete. In the second chart, I treated resident abortion rate as the only outcome that mattered and collapsed several forms of access restriction into one burden score, which made the overall pattern read as “higher barriers, lower rates.”

This assignment made clear that the most persuasive manipulations are often not the loudest ones. They are quieter decisions: which states to show, which variable to foreground, what to name the chart, how to sort the cases, and where to direct visual attention through color. In my first chart, I used darker color to attract the eye toward the values I most wanted the viewer to notice. In my second chart, I used the lower-right quadrant, darker tones, and a red palette to make one region feel like the natural conclusion of the figure. These choices do not fabricate data, but they shape where the viewer looks and what they take away first.

The line between persuasion and deception, then, is not just about whether the data are real. It is also about whether the visual system leaves room for competing interpretations to remain visible. Both of my charts are grounded in the same data, but each one quietly privileges a different understanding of what the data “mean.” That is what makes this assignment both powerful and ethically uncomfortable: the strongest visual arguments are often built not from falsehood, but from selective visibility.
