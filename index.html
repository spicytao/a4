Interactive Visualization and Society · Assignment 4

Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

This report uses the same Guttmacher Institute state-level abortion dataset to construct two opposed visual arguments. Rather than treating visualization as neutral reporting, I treat it as a rhetorical practice shaped by data selection, transformation, emphasis, and framing.

Shengtao Shen sstheo@mit.edu A4 Guttmacher Institute Abortion Data by State

Proposition

Proposition

Abortion bans and clinic scarcity do not reduce abortion need; they primarily relocate where abortion care is recorded.

Visualization 1 — For the Proposition

The first visualization argues that low in-state abortion counts are not evidence of low need. Instead, the most revealing signal is the gap between abortions occurring inside a state and abortions obtained by that state’s residents.

Design rationale

How this visualization was made persuasive

This figure persuades by redefining what should count as the meaningful measure. If the viewer only sees abortions that happened inside a state, low values can look like a decline in need. By juxtaposing those values with the abortion rate among residents of the same state, the chart instead suggests that need persists even when care has become geographically displaced.

The argument depends on both an earnest analytical move and a selective rhetorical one. The earnest move is the comparison itself: resident rate and occurrence rate are genuinely different measures, and comparing them surfaces an important policy distinction. The selective move is that I center the states where this discrepancy is strongest, sort them by gap size, and visually emphasize the metric I want the reader to trust most. The chart therefore does not invent a pattern, but it does organize the page so that one interpretation becomes much easier to see than competing alternatives.

  • Score: +1.5 Comparing resident rate to in-state occurrence rate. This is the most important analytical choice in the entire project, and I consider it largely earnest. The two measures answer genuinely different questions, and placing them in the same frame helps reveal that “where abortions are recorded” is not the same as “whether residents seek abortions.” It makes the proposition legible without distorting the underlying values.
  • Score: -1.5 Filtering to states with the largest mismatch. I did not show all states equally. Instead, I selected the cases where the difference between resident and in-state rates is strongest, because those are the states that best support the proposition. This is persuasive because it compresses the reader’s attention around the strongest evidence, but it is also selective because smaller or contradictory cases disappear.
  • Score: -1 Sorting by gap size rather than alphabetically or regionally. Ordering the states from the largest mismatch downward turns a collection of examples into an apparent ranked pattern. This makes the proposition feel systematic and cumulative. The move is subtle, but it strongly shapes what the reader notices first.
  • Score: -0.5 Using a span-based encoding rather than two detached points or bars. The span is analytically useful because it makes the difference between the two rates immediately visible. At the same time, it also dramatizes the gap: long horizontal distances feel consequential in a way that separate bars might not. I see this as a mixed choice—honest in structure, but rhetorically strong in effect.
  • Score: -1.5 Color hierarchy and title framing. I intentionally made the resident-related endpoint darker and visually heavier, so the viewer’s eye is pulled toward the measure I want to count as reality. The title goes a step further by interpreting the gap as “relocation,” which is a plausible reading but still a stronger causal claim than the marks alone can prove. Together, color and language quietly steer interpretation before the reader begins to evaluate the data on their own.

Visualization 2 — Against the Proposition

The second visualization argues the opposite by changing the frame of analysis. Rather than highlighting displacement between two measures, it treats resident abortion rate as the central outcome and asks whether greater access barriers still align with lower rates.

Design rationale

How this visualization was made persuasive

This chart works by suppressing the comparison that was central in Figure 1. Once the reader is no longer asked to compare in-state and resident rates, the relocation story recedes. In its place, a new story becomes dominant: states with heavier burdens of restriction tend to sit lower on resident abortion rate.

Much of the persuasion here comes from constructed coherence. I combine multiple restriction variables into one burden score, add a regression line to impose directional order on a noisy cloud, and use quadrant shading so that the lower-right corner becomes the chart’s rhetorical destination. These moves are not fabricated, but they simplify a more ambiguous field of evidence into a cleaner anti-proposition narrative.

  • Score: -1.5 Using resident abortion rate as the only outcome variable. This is persuasive because it removes the in-state versus resident mismatch that made displacement visible in Figure 1. The reader is no longer invited to ask where abortions occurred, only whether rates are lower in high-barrier states. That reframing is legitimate, but strategically narrow.
  • Score: +0.5 Constructing a composite Access Burden Score. This is partly earnest and partly rhetorical. On the earnest side, combining multiple restrictions into a single index is a defensible analytical transformation that creates a more holistic variable than any one rule or provider count alone. On the rhetorical side, the score appears authoritative and unified, which can make a contested construction feel more natural than it really is.
  • Score: -1 Adding a regression line. The regression line gives the viewer an immediate takeaway and reduces the apparent ambiguity of the scatterplot. It is not false, but it encourages the eye to read trend before dispersion. This makes the anti-position feel stronger and cleaner than the raw cloud of points would on its own.
  • Score: -1.5 Darkening the lower-right quadrant and its points. I specifically wanted the reader to focus on the region representing high burden and low resident rate, so I made that area darker both in the background and in the marks. This channels attention toward the examples that best support the anti-claim. The data are unchanged, but the hierarchy of attention is deliberately unequal.
  • Score: -1 Selective labeling and affective red framing. I only label a subset of states, chosen because they help stabilize the chart’s directional reading. The red palette also adds urgency and consequence, making the emphasized region feel like the place where the “real” conclusion lies. These are subtle persuasive choices rather than outright falsifications.

Final Reflection

What changed between these two visualizations was not the dataset, but the definition of what counted as evidence and the social role I asked the chart to play. In Figure 1, I foregrounded the gap between resident and in-state abortion rates, which made displacement visible and made low in-state values look incomplete. In Figure 2, I changed both the dependent variable and the explanatory frame, collapsing multiple restrictions into a burden score and asking the reader to interpret resident abortion rate as the primary outcome. The same dataset therefore supported two opposed narratives not because one was fabricated, but because each chart selectively elevated a different structure within the data.

This process made me much less confident that there is a stable formal boundary between “persuasive” and “deceptive” visualization. Some of my choices were clearly strategic, such as filtering, ordering, selective labeling, and using color hierarchy to pull attention toward the evidence that best supported each side. But other choices felt genuinely informative: comparing two distinct abortion measures in Figure 1, or constructing a burden index in Figure 2 to summarize multiple access restrictions in one interpretable variable. What unsettled me is that the same technique can look ethical or manipulative depending on context, transparency, and audience. A transformation can be both analytically meaningful and rhetorically loaded. A title can help readers understand a point, but also silently pre-interpret it. Visualization is therefore not just a matter of showing data; it is a matter of staging a relationship between evidence, author, and reader.

The images below helped me think about that relationship more clearly. They suggest that a visualization is rarely “just” information. It can be an invitation to notice, a prompt to act, a lure into discussion, or even a reflection of the creator’s own socio-cultural position. In other words, a chart nearly always presupposes a recipient and imagines a response. Once I started thinking this way, I realized that my own charts were never neutral containers. Each one anticipated a viewer, guided their attention, and quietly hoped they would leave with a particular interpretation. Ethical visualization, then, may not mean eliminating rhetoric altogether; it may mean recognizing that rhetoric is always present, and taking responsibility for how strongly, how selectively, and how invisibly we deploy it.

My takeaway from this assignment is that ethical visualization is not the absence of persuasion, but the disciplined use of it. I no longer think the most important question is whether a chart is “biased” in the abstract, because every act of selection, transformation, and emphasis introduces perspective. The more useful question is whether the designer leaves enough of the data visible for alternative interpretations to remain possible, or whether the visualization closes down ambiguity so aggressively that its rhetoric starts to masquerade as inevitability.

That is why some of my choices above are scored as mixed rather than purely deceptive. A calculated field, a composite index, or a comparison between two measures can be both analytically sincere and strategically useful. The ethical stakes emerge not from the existence of framing, but from how much that framing conceals its own contingency. In that sense, the line between acceptable persuasion and misleading design is real but unstable: it is less a border we can draw once and for all than a responsibility we have to renegotiate each time we turn data into a visual argument.
